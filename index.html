A field guide to the evidence

Read the evidence, never the words.

At band 8 the test is no longer about vocabulary — it is about proof. Every answer is licensed by a specific span of text, dressed up in synonyms so the surface no longer matches the question. This casebook trains the one move that separates 6.5 from 8: locating that span, and reading what it actually claims.

00 · Foundations

The band 8 mindset

Sixty minutes, three passages, forty answers, no extra transfer time. Band 8 is roughly 35/40 — you can miss five and still hit target, but you cannot afford to drown in one hard passage. Pace and evidence discipline matter more than raw speed.

20min
per passage

Hard cap. If you hit 20 minutes, transfer your best answers and move on. A passage left unstarted is a guaranteed zero.

~90sec
per question

Average budget. Spend less on locating-type questions, bank the surplus for inference MCQs.

0min
transfer time

Unlike Listening, you write straight onto the answer sheet. Never leave it to the end.

35/40
band 8 target

Indicative. The third passage is usually hardest — protect time for it, don’t starve it.

P-01

The answer is in the text

IELTS Reading never rewards outside knowledge or opinion. If you can’t point to the exact line, you don’t have the answer yet — you have a guess.

P-02

Match meaning, not words

Keyword-spotting is a 6.0 habit. The test author paraphrases the answer and plants the original keyword in a wrong option. Read for the proposition.

P-03

Most answers run in order

For most question sets the answers follow the passage top-to-bottom. Use the previous answer’s location to bound your search for the next.

P-04

Don’t over-read

You do not need to understand every word. You need to find the licensing sentence and read that precisely. Skim to locate, slow down to decide.

P1 · The core skill

The paraphrase engine

Every question is the answer sentence run through a disguise machine. Learn the transformations and you can run them backwards — from the question’s wording to the text’s. These are the swaps examiners use again and again.

a rise in numbers → a growing population noun → noun phrase · synonym
scientists discovered → it was revealed by researchers active → passive · agent moved
cheaper to produce → production costs fell adjective → noun (nominalisation)
few people supported it → it met with little enthusiasm negative quantifier · litotes
the plan failed → the scheme did not achieve its aims verb → negated antonym
unlike birds, bats … → whereas birds …, bats by contrast … contrast marker swap
Drill

When you meet a question, underline its 2–3 content words and immediately ask: “What are three other ways to say this?” You are now scanning for meaning, not letters. The matched words in the text will rarely be the ones in the question.

P2 · Reading the fine print

Hard sentence patterns

These structures decide True vs False, the right MCQ option, and whether a claim is the author’s own. Misreading one of them is the most common way a strong reader loses a mark. Slow down whenever you see them.

01 Concession (although / despite)

Although the drug reduced symptoms, it did not extend survival.

The main clause carries the real point. The drug did NOT extend survival — the concession clause is a decoy that sounds positive.

02 Double negative / litotes

The findings were not insignificant.

Two negatives = a positive. This means the findings were significant. IELTS loves not uncommon, by no means certain.

03 Hedging & modality

The evidence suggests temperatures may have risen.

Suggests / may = tentative, not fact. A statement saying “temperatures rose” as certain fact can be Not Given or even False.

04 Attribution (who said it)

Critics argued the theory was flawed; Hassan rejected this view.

Track whose claim it is. The author may report a view without endorsing it. MCQs test exactly this confusion.

05 Restrictive qualifiers

Only in coastal regions did the species recover.

Only / except / unless / apart from shrink the scope. A statement dropping the qualifier (“the species recovered”) over-generalises and is usually False.

06 Comparatives & superlatives

No other method proved as reliable.

= this method was the most reliable. Watch more than, as … as, the least — a flipped comparison is a classic False.

07 Cause vs correlation

Sales rose after the campaign, though the link is unclear.

The text declines to claim cause. A statement saying the campaign caused the rise adds a link the text denies — Not Given/False.

08 Conditionals

Had funding continued, the project would have succeeded.

A hypothetical, not a record of events. It tells you the project did not continue and did not succeed.

P3 · Know the enemy

The trap taxonomy

Wrong options aren’t random — they’re engineered. Each belongs to a family. Once you can name the trap in front of you, it stops working. These recur across every question type.

  • The keyword magnet. An option reuses a striking word from the passage verbatim while distorting the meaning. The familiar word feels safe; that’s the bait. Passage: “the theory was eventually abandoned.” Wrong option: “the theory was abandoned at once.”
  • The extreme upgrade. The text says some / often / may; the option says all / always / proves. Absolute language is right far less often than it appears. Text: “many experts believe” → trap: “all experts agree”.
  • False vs Not Given. False = the text says the opposite. Not Given = the text simply never addresses it. If you’re inferring “it’s probably false” from world knowledge, it’s Not Given.
  • The partial match. Half the option is supported, half isn’t. Both halves must be true. One unsupported clause sinks the whole option.
  • The attribution swap. The option assigns a view to the wrong person, or treats a reported opinion as the author’s own fact.
  • Out of scope / time shift. The option is true in general or in a different period than the one the text discusses. Right fact, wrong frame.
  • The plausible inference. A sensible real-world conclusion the text never actually licenses. Reasonable ≠ stated. No line, no answer.
P4 · The raw material

Synonym banks

The paraphrase engine needs fuel. These are the high-frequency meanings examiners disguise, grouped by sense — because in the exam you search by what a word means, not its first letter. Type a word to find its family.

P5 · Precision traps

Easily confused pairs

At band 8 a single wrong gloss flips a True into a False. These look-alikes and sound-alikes are precisely the words examiners lean on. Learn the one-line discriminator for each.

No match — try the other half of the pair.

P6 · The way it’s phrased

Academic collocations

Academic passages are built from fixed partnerships, not free word choice. Recognising the chunk — pose a threat, draw a conclusion, a body of evidence — speeds up reading and flags where claims and answers sit.

Write to the criteria, not to a template.

00 · The rules of the game

How Writing is scored

Each task is marked on four equally-weighted criteria. Your task band is their average; the two tasks are then combined (Task 2 double-weighted) for the Writing band. You can’t hide a weak criterion — band 8 means hitting all four.

W-01

Answer the whole task

Cover every part of the prompt, take a clear position, and stay on topic. Task 1 needs a data overview; Task 2 needs a developed answer to the exact question.

W-02

Structure is visible

Clear paragraphs, one idea each, joined naturally. The examiner should see your plan without effort — and not feel the joins are mechanical.

W-03

Precision over rarity

A precise, correctly-collocated common word beats a misused rare one. Flexibility and accuracy score; thesaurus-dumping does not.

W-04

Vary sentences on purpose

A mix of simple and complex structures, mostly error-free. Range with control — not long tangled sentences for show.

A conversation you can prepare for

Speak to communicate, not to perform.

The examiner isn’t grading your ideas — they’re grading how you say them: your flow, your words, your grammar and your sound. Band 8 is a relaxed, well-structured talker who extends every answer, varies their language on purpose, and is easy to follow. This half of the casebook turns each part into a repeatable plan — and a way to train it.

4–5min
Part 1 · interview

Short questions on familiar topics. Answer in 2–4 sentences — never one word.

1–2min
Part 2 · long turn

One minute to prepare, then you speak alone. Structure beats inspiration.

4–5min
Part 3 · discussion

Abstract, two-way. Where band 7+ is won — opinion, reasons, balance.

~14min
face to face

One examiner, recorded. Four criteria, each worth 25% of your band.

00 · How to play it

The Listening test

Four short habits decide most of your Listening score — long before vocabulary does.

L-01

It plays once

There is no replay and no going back. If you miss one, let it go and lock onto the next — don’t lose two questions chasing one.

L-02

Read ahead

In Parts 3–4 the questions are printed. Read them during the directions and pauses so you know exactly what to listen for.

L-03

Never leave blanks

There is no penalty for a wrong answer. If unsure, eliminate and guess — always fill a bubble.

L-04

Predict, then confirm

Use the photo / question to pre-load likely vocabulary, then listen to confirm rather than decode from scratch.

00 · How to play it

The Reading test

Reading is a race against the clock. These four habits keep you from running out.

R-01

Bank time early

Spend ~20–30s per Part 5 item and move. The minutes you save are what get you through Part 7.

R-02

Protect Part 7

Part 7 is 54 questions. Leave it ~50–55 minutes; never let Parts 5–6 eat that time.

R-03

Never leave blanks

No penalty for wrong answers — bubble every question, even the ones you skip and guess.

R-04

Questions first, then scan

Read the questions, then hunt the text by paraphrase. Flag a hard item and keep moving.
